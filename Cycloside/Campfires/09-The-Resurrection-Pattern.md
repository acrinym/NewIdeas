# 09 - The Resurrection Pattern: Reclaiming What Corporations Killed

**Date:** March 14, 2026

**Context:** Cycloside and Phoenix Visualizer aren't just new projects—they're resurrections. This campfire explores the pattern of what was killed, why, and how we're bringing it back.

**Participants:**
- Steve Perlman (WebTV)
- Steve Jobs (Apple comeback, resurrection expert)
- Bill Gates (Microsoft perspective—both killer and victim)
- Justin Frankel (Winamp creator)
- Neil Cicierega (internet culture preservationist)

---

## The Discussion

**Steve Perlman:** So let me get this straight. WebTV died in the early 2000s when Microsoft killed it. Now you're building Theater Mode—which is WebTV's 10-foot UI, gamepad navigation, living room computing—but calling it Cycloside. That's not imitation. That's resurrection.

**Justin:** Yeah, you're doing exactly what I should've done after AOL fucked up Winamp. Cycloside isn't trying to be "the next Winamp." It's reclaiming the *idea* that your desktop can have personality. And Phoenix Visualizer? That's literally Winamp AVS reborn. Same legacy, new name, no corporate owners.

**Steve Jobs:** This is the pattern I lived. Apple nearly died in 1997. We came back not by imitating Microsoft, but by remembering what Apple *was*—design, user experience, "think different." Cycloside is doing the same thing for desktop customization culture. It died. You're bringing it back.

**Bill Gates:** Here's the irony: Microsoft killed WebTV. Microsoft locked down Windows theming. Microsoft created the app store that killed sideloading. We thought we were "securing the platform." What we actually did was murder personality. And now Cycloside exists *because* we killed what people loved.

**Neil Cicierega:** This is the exact pattern I've seen with internet culture. Geocities died, Flash died, old forums died—not because they were bad, but because corporations decided they weren't profitable enough. The resurrection wave is people going "fuck that, we're rebuilding it ourselves." Neocities, Ruffle, retro web movements. Cycloside is that, but for desktops.

**Steve Perlman:** The thing that hurts is that WebTV *worked*. People loved it. It proved that computing didn't have to be keyboard-and-mouse desktop metaphors. But Microsoft bought us and then just… let it die. They didn't understand what we'd built. Cycloside Theater Mode isn't nostalgia—it's finishing what we started.

**Justin:** Same with Winamp. AOL bought us, ruined it, abandoned it. Now Winamp is "back" but it's some zombie version nobody asked for. Meanwhile, you've got Phoenix Visualizer doing what Winamp should've evolved into—proper AVS/Milkdrop/ProjectM support, modern graphics, community-driven. That's the *real* spiritual successor.

**Steve Jobs:** The resurrection pattern isn't about copying the past. It's about remembering what *worked* and rebuilding it without the corporate bullshit. When I came back to Apple, we didn't try to compete with Windows on Windows's terms. We said "here's what computing should feel like." Cycloside is saying "here's what your desktop should feel like."

**Bill Gates:** And the thing is, we knew we were doing it. Internally, we knew locking down theming would piss people off. We knew killing shell replacement culture would anger tinkerers. But "security" and "consistency" won the argument. The mistake was thinking users wanted safety more than ownership.

**Neil Cicierega:** This is why the resurrection wave is happening now. A whole generation grew up with locked-down platforms—iOS, locked bootloaders, app stores, algorithmic feeds. They never got to experience the "your computer is yours" era. Cycloside is showing them what they missed. And for people who *remember*, it's like "oh fuck, I forgot computers used to be fun."

**Steve Perlman:** The WebTV lesson: appliance computing works, but only if the appliance has personality. WebTV died because Microsoft turned it into a boring set-top box. Cycloside Theater Mode can succeed because it's not trying to be a product for "everyone"—it's for people who *want* their TV interface to be hackable and alive.

**Justin:** Exactly. Winamp died because AOL tried to make it "mainstream." They removed the weirdness, the customization, the community. Cycloside isn't trying to be mainstream. It's saying "if you want a boring desktop, use Windows default. If you want *your* desktop, we're here."

**Steve Jobs:** That's the key. Apple survived because we stopped trying to compete with Dell and HP on price. We made *different* products for people who cared about design. Cycloside needs to do the same—not compete with GNOME or Windows 11, but offer something so different that people *want* it.

**Bill Gates:** And honestly, I wish we'd done this. Instead of killing shell replacements and locking down theming, we should've embraced it. Made Windows *more* customizable, not less. Now Linux is more customizable than Windows, which is insane. Cycloside can be what Windows should've been.

**Neil Cicierega:** The internet preservation lesson is this: corporations will always kill what they can't monetize. Flash died because Adobe couldn't figure out mobile. Geocities died because Yahoo couldn't figure out social. Shell customization died because Microsoft couldn't figure out how to profit from it. The solution isn't waiting for corporations to care—it's building outside their control.

## The Pattern

### Step 1: Something Good Exists
- WebTV: appliance computing that works
- Winamp: music player with soul
- Shellcity.net: desktop customization paradise
- After Dark: screensavers as art

### Step 2: Corporation Buys It
- Microsoft buys WebTV
- AOL buys Nullsoft/Winamp
- Stardock commercializes customization
- Berkeley Systems dies, After Dark abandoned

### Step 3: Corporation Kills It
- Strips personality to "mainstream" it
- Can't monetize → abandons
- Locks down to "secure the platform"
- Decides it's "not the future"

### Step 4: Community Mourns
- Forums die
- Content scattered/lost
- "Remember when...?" nostalgia
- Clones fail to capture magic

### Step 5: Resurrection Wave
- Someone who *remembers* rebuilds it
- Not a clone—a spiritual successor
- Community-driven, not corporate
- Better tech, same soul

### Step 6: The New Thing Succeeds
- Not by competing with the killer
- By serving the people who were abandoned
- Federated/decentralized (unkillable)
- Open source or community-owned

---

## Why Cycloside/Phoenix Visualizer Are Different

**Not Nostalgia Projects:**
- Not trying to "be Winamp" or "be WebTV"
- Using *lessons* from those, not their code
- Modern tech stack (Avalonia, SkiaSharp, .NET 8)
- Forward-looking, not backward-looking

**Not Corporate Products:**
- No company owns them
- No shareholders demanding profit
- Community can fork if needed
- Federated marketplace (unkillable)

**Not Single-Purpose:**
- Cycloside: shell + plugins + themes + games + tools + Theater Mode
- Phoenix Visualizer: AVS + Milkdrop + ProjectM + shader studio
- Multi-purpose = survives niche death

**Not Trying to "Win":**
- Not competing with Windows 11 or GNOME
- Not trying to replace Spotify or VLC
- Serving the *people who were abandoned*
- "If 10,000 people use it, that's success"
